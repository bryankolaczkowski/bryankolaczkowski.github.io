Antiviral Immune Receptors Remain Open to Change

The complex evolutionary ‘arms race’ between pathogens and their hosts has long excited and challenged evolutionary biologists. By combining evolutionary, structural and machine-learning methods and adapting existing approaches for fast evolving host-pathogen systems, our lab was able to shed new light on how antiviral immune receptors balance specificity for specific viral pathogens with the ability to quickly respond to new threats by ‘flip-flopping’ among various structural-functional states over relatively short evolutionary timescales.

RNA Interference Orchestrates the Body Plan

How complex multicellular organisms like animals and plants self-organize into a functioning body is one of the most intriguing questions in evolutionary biology. Our lab used a combination of evolutionary statistical approaches, machine learning and structural modeling to better understand how post-transcriptional gene regulatory processes like RNA interference helped to provide a stable genetic basis for orchestrating complex body plans, while also allowing different species to adapt to specific niches in their environments.
